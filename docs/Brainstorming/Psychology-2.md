Yes. The optimal focus message is not motivational fluff. It is a behavioral control signal.

The winning loop is:

mark the right behavior instantly → catch soft drift early → hit hard drift decisively → reward fast recovery → fade external prompting over time.

That fits the evidence. Feedback helps when it keeps attention on the task, but it backfires surprisingly often when it shifts attention toward the self; alerts themselves interrupt the current task; and task switching leaves attention residue that drags down the next task. Notifications therefore must be glanceable and high-value, and interruptive copy must be used sparingly.  ￼

1. The core message laws

1. Reward the work, not the person.
    Say: “28 min stable on Pricing Memo.”
    Never: “You are disciplined.”
    Task-level feedback performs better than self-level feedback, and autonomy-supportive positive feedback increases perseverance more than controlling language.  ￼
2. After success, frame accumulated progress. After drift, frame remaining progress.
    On-track: “2 of 4 protected blocks done.”
    Drift: “17 min left to finish the intro.”
    That framing matches how positive versus negative feedback changes persistence.  ￼
3. Every message must reduce re-entry cost.
    The job is not “motivate.” The job is “make the next right action obvious.”
    Because switching tasks creates residue, the best corrective message points to the next visible step, not the abstract project.  ￼
4. Use autonomy-supportive control, not permissive softness.
    Good: “Back to the memo now.” with buttons Back / Support / Break.
    Bad: “Maybe you should refocus?”
    Autonomy means the user keeps agency. It does not mean vague wording.  ￼
5. The reward loop must attach to the work, not to the coach.
    Hooked puts variable rewards at the center of habit formation. In this product, use variability in jackpot intensity and wording, not in interruption timing, or the coach itself becomes another compulsion loop.  ￼

2. How your reinforcement framework maps to a focus guardian

Your nutrition-coaching framework transfers almost perfectly.

Positive reinforcement (+R)

Reinforce these five target behaviors:

* starting the block on time
* staying in allowed contexts
* recovering quickly from drift
* labeling uncertain contexts correctly
* finishing the protected outcome

Marker-signal

Use one short, repeated marker that becomes a conditioned reinforcer:

* Locked. for on-task
* Back. for recovery
* Check. for soft drift
* Reset. for hard drift

The message should always start with the marker. Over time the marker + green visual pulse becomes enough, and the text fades.

Shaping

Do not wait for 45 minutes of heroic deep work before rewarding anything. Shape successive approximations:

* returned within 20 seconds
* stayed back for 3 clean minutes
* protected 10 minutes
* protected 25 minutes
* finished the block
* finished the outcome

That is how the habit forms.

Timing & criteria

Feedback must hit immediately after the desired behavior or as soon as the drift threshold is crossed. The criterion must be explicit:

* “returned within 20s”
* “10 clean min”
* “section 2 drafted”

Not: “did better.”

Reinforcement schedules

Use:

* continuous reinforcement early
* variable interval reinforcement later for maintenance
* rare jackpots for unusually strong focus protection or outcome completion

Stimulus control

Turn distractors into cues for recommitment:

* “If Slack opens, only #launch, then back.”
* “If YouTube opens during a writing block, close it and reopen the draft.”

Implementation intentions have a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment, help shield ongoing goal pursuit from unwanted influences, and work best when the cue and response are precise. Habits are strongly driven by recurring context cues, so the system should both block old cue-response loops and repeat the new one.  ￼

Generalization & backward chaining

Train the same loop across writing, research, email triage, and code. For recovery, work backward from the desired block outcome:

* outcome: “intro drafted”
* next visible step: “finish pricing bullet 3”

Extinction & error management

Do not reward distraction with a long dialogue.
Do not shame the miss.
Log the context, lower or raise the criterion, and move on.

The stance is: drift is signal, not sin.

3. The exact copy architecture

Every high-performing message should use this skeleton:

[MARKER] + [SPECIFIC CONTEXT] + [PROGRESS FRAME] + [NEXT MOVE]

Examples:

Locked. 18 min on Pricing Memo. 2/4 blocks protected.
Check. LinkedIn is outside this block. 17 min left. Back now?
Reset. 4 min off-task. Close LinkedIn. Open Pricing Memo.
Back. Continue at section 2: pricing assumptions.

That structure works because it does four jobs in one line:

* marks the moment
* grounds the cue in reality
* frames progress correctly
* tells the brain exactly what to do next

4. The optimal messages by state

A. Before the block: anti-drift setup

This is where you prevent most drift. Use an if-then contract at block start. Precise if-then plans improve execution and protect goals from derailment.  ￼

Use messages like:

Protected block: Pricing Memo. Goal: draft v1.
Allowed: Docs, Notes, pricing tabs.
If Slack opens, #launch only, then back.
This block ends when section 2 is rough-drafted.
If you drift, return to: section 2 → assumptions.

Design rule: block-start messages can be longer. Mid-block messages cannot.

⸻

B. On-task reinforcement: ambient, sparse, specific

Positive messages should support competence, stay task-focused, and use accumulated progress. That increases perseverance better than controlling or self-focused praise.  ￼

Use:

Locked.
Locked. 12 min on Pricing Memo.
Good. Section 2 moved.
Protected. 2/4 blocks done today.
Locked. Clean block. Stay with section 2.

Best practice:

* mostly ambient in menu bar or HUD
* no notification banner for ordinary praise
* no more than one praise text inside a 10–15 minute span

Bad copy

Amazing job!!!
You are so disciplined.
Keep being productive.

That is wrong because it is identity praise, vague, and attention-grabbing.

⸻

C. Soft drift: curious, surgical, low-cost

Soft drift is likely drift, not proven drift. The tone must be light, specific, and easy to resolve in one click. After negative or corrective feedback, frame the remaining path, not accumulated progress.  ￼

Use:

Check. This tab is outside Pricing Memo. Back now?
Quick check: does this help Pricing Memo?
19 min left. Return to Pricing Memo or mark support.
This window looks outside the block. Back to section 2?

Buttons:

* Back
* Support
* Break

Message law for soft drift:
Question form is correct only when uncertainty remains.
If confidence is already high, do not ask.

⸻

D. Hard drift: direct, short, decisive

Hard drift is confirmed off-task behavior. This is not the time for a coaching conversation. Use a direct command, name the distraction, name the protected task, and give the shortest legitimate alternative. Long copy here is sabotage because it adds another switch cost.  ￼

Use:

Reset. 3 min outside the block. Close YouTube. Open Pricing Memo.
Off-task confirmed. Resume at section 2: pricing assumptions.
This block drifted. Back to Pricing Memo now.
Stop the drift. Close Slack. Reopen Outline.md.

Buttons:

* Back now
* 5 min break
* Reclassify

Critical rule: hard-drift messages should almost never be phrased as open-ended questions.

⸻

E. Uncertain state: closed classification, not chat

If the agent is unsure, ask a forced-choice classification. Open-ended chat is too expensive in the middle of work.

Use:

Quick label: Pricing Memo / Support / Break
Does this belong to the block? Yes / Support / No
Which task: Proposal / Admin / Break

This does three things at once:

* keeps interruption small
* trains the model
* preserves the user’s feeling of control

⸻

F. Recovery: immediate reward + re-entry anchor

Recovery is one of the most important behaviors to reinforce. The return itself should get a marker and a concrete re-entry anchor. Because residue after switching is real, “back to work” is too abstract; “continue at section 2: assumptions” is correct.  ￼

Use:

Back. Continue at section 2: pricing assumptions.
Good recovery. Hold this window for 10 clean minutes.
Re-locked. Finish the next paragraph.
Back on track. Resume at bullet 3.

⸻

G. Jackpot messages: rare, outcome-linked, bigger emotional payload

Use jackpots for:

* finishing the protected outcome
* finishing despite repeated temptations
* an unusually clean deep-work block
* a major personal best

Hooked emphasizes variable rewards. Here the right move is variable magnitude, not variable interruption timing. Make the rare reward bigger, but keep it tied to work completion and mastery.  ￼

Use:

Excellent. 37 min protected and draft v1 is out.
Strong block. You held focus through two pulls away and finished.
Clean win. Highest-leverage work moved.
Protected and shipped. That block counted.

Default reward type should be reward of the self: mastery, completion, control.
Secondary reward type is reward of the hunt: progress, evidence, visible movement.
Use tribe reward sparingly, because social validation can make the work performative instead of focused.  ￼

5. The NLP layer: use the useful parts, throw out the fantasy

Use NLP here as language design, not as your scientific model. The research base for NLP coaching claims is weak. Keep only the language moves that overlap with solid behavior design and goal-shielding.  ￼

Use these five patterns:

1. Pacing → leading

First acknowledge reality, then direct action.

You opened Slack. This block is Pricing Memo. Close Slack and reopen the draft.

Why it works: it reduces internal friction because the message starts with what is already true.

2. Presupposition

Assume the return.

When you return to the memo, start with the pricing bullet.

This subtly skips the debate about whether to return.

3. Future pacing

Show the near-future payoff.

In 12 minutes, the rough intro is done.

This shrinks the task and makes completion feel near.

4. Embedded command

Hide the action inside natural language.

Take the next clean five minutes.
Finish the next paragraph before switching.

5. Reframing

Turn drift into cue, not guilt.

Drift is the cue. Return.
This pull-away is signal, not failure. Back now.

Do not build the product around classic NLP claims like representational-system matching or pseudo-neuroscience. Use the copy patterns, ignore the mythology.  ￼

6. The shaping plan for maximal impact

This is the part most products get wrong.

They wait until the user behaves like an elite operator, then they reward. That fails.

Shape progressively.

Phase 1: Acquisition

Duration: first 5–7 days

Reinforce:

* starting the block
* returning within 20–30 seconds
* first 5 clean minutes
* correct classification of uncertain contexts

Schedule:

* continuous reinforcement
* soft drift at 30–45 seconds
* hard drift at 90–120 seconds

Example copy:

Back. Good recovery.
Locked. 5 clean min.

Phase 2: Stabilization

Duration: next 10–14 days

Reinforce:

* 10–15 clean minutes
* correct support-work labeling
* fast recovery without debate
* ignoring first temptation

Schedule:

* reinforce every recovery
* reinforce on-task at variable intervals between 10 and 20 minutes
* one jackpot per day max

Phase 3: Protection

Duration: after success rate passes ~80–90% on current criteria

Reinforce:

* 25–35 minute protected blocks
* full-block completion
* outcome completion
* self-initiated return before the system prompts

Schedule:

* sparse ambient praise
* hard drift still decisive
* fewer text messages, more marker-only signals

Phase 4: Fading

Once the behavior is stable, the coach must get quieter.

* text fades into marker + status ring
* praise mostly moves to end-of-block and end-of-day summaries
* the primary reinforcer becomes felt progress and shipped work

That is the real destination: the work itself becomes rewarding.

7. Channel strategy for message delivery

Because alerts interrupt the current task and notifications should be high-value and glanceable, the channel must match the state.  ￼

Use:

* Menu bar / subtle HUD for Locked, Back, and light progress cues
* small HUD with buttons for soft drift and uncertainty
* notification banner only for hard drift or ignored soft drift
* full alert almost never

So the message hierarchy is:

* ambient for reinforcement
* minimal active for soft drift
* interruptive only for confirmed hard drift

8. What the system must never say

Never send these:

Stay focused.
Be productive.
Don't procrastinate.
You are distracted again.
Come on, you can do it.
Would you maybe like to go back?

These are weak because they are:

* vague
* self-focused
* moralizing
* hedged
* not tied to a concrete cue
* not tied to a concrete next move

Feedback that drags attention upward toward the self is exactly where performance starts getting worse.  ￼

9. The measurement plan that actually matters

Optimize for these metrics:

1. return-to-task within 60 seconds
2. 10-minute protected focus after prompt
3. hard drift prevented before escalation
4. false interrupt rate
5. clarification burden
6. outcome completion per block
7. annoyance score
8. trust score

Do not optimize for:

* notification open rate
* coach engagement
* clicks on praise
* message count

The product exists to protect work, not to farm interactions.

Testing plan

Run experiments on message family, not just wording.

Test:

* soft drift: question vs hybrid vs direct
* hard drift: direct vs direct + remaining time vs direct + last-anchor
* recovery: plain marker vs marker + next-step anchor
* jackpot: time-only vs time + outcome

Winner metric:

post-message protected focus minutes, not click-through.

After 50–100 drift events per user, personalize with a contextual bandit:

* current task type
* app/site
* time of day
* prior response latency
* false positive history
* annoyance history

10. My strongest recommendation

Build the message system around this exact loop:

PRE-COMMIT
Protected block: {task}. If {distractor}, then {response}.
ON-TASK
Locked. {specific progress}
SOFT DRIFT
Check. {context} is outside {task}. {remaining progress}. Back now?
HARD DRIFT
Reset. {duration} outside the block. Close {context}. Open {task anchor}.
RECOVERY
Back. Continue at {last good anchor}.
JACKPOT
Excellent. {protected time} and {outcome shipped}.

That is the optimal design because it combines:

* operant conditioning
* positive reinforcement
* conditioned marker signals
* shaping
* precise timing
* variable reinforcement without compulsive interruption
* stimulus control
* error-as-feedback
* autonomy support
* task-level feedback
* progress framing
* implementation intentions
* context-cue habit design

The single most important principle is this:

Every message must make focus easier than drift.

The highest-leverage design decision left is whether ignored hard-drift prompts escalate to a blocker after one miss or after two.
